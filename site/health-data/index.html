Health Sovereignty

Your data, not a population average

Conventional medicine treats you as the statistical average patient. Your body isn't average.

This page is deliberately not about supplements or cure claims — it's about tools that show you real data about your own body (glucose response, biomarkers, recovery, sleep) so you can make informed decisions and have a genuinely useful conversation with a doctor, instead of guessing or accepting a one-size-fits-all protocol. Every product here operates within FDA's "general wellness" framework — data and information, not diagnosis or treatment — and we've flagged the two products in this space that have run into regulatory trouble for crossing that exact line.

Why Whoop isn't listed as a pick here

Whoop received an FDA warning letter in July 2025 stating its "Blood Pressure Insights" feature functioned as an unapproved medical device — the exact line this page is built to stay on the safe side of. It's also facing an active privacy class action alleging vitals and video-viewing data were shared with third parties without consent, and drew backlash in 2025 for charging existing subscribers $49–79 to upgrade hardware. None of this makes Whoop hardware bad at what it measures, but it's a live example of a company in this exact category crossing the data-vs-diagnosis line, and worth knowing about rather than recommending uncritically.

The rule this whole page follows

The FDA's own guidance draws a real, specific line: a product intended solely for general wellness — helping you see and track something about your body, not diagnose or treat a named disease — is treated very differently than a product marketed to diagnose, treat, mitigate, or cure a condition. That's the difference between "this shows you your glucose response to a meal" (fine) and "this treats your blood sugar" (regulated, and the reason a well-known natural-health publisher was hit with a $5.3 million FTC settlement and an FDA warning letter for exactly that kind of language). Every product on this page stays on the data side of that line, and where a company has crossed it or come close (Whoop's blood-pressure feature, Berkey's unverified claims), we've said so directly instead of leaving it out quietly.
